Tenets of Cerebrovascular Injury

  1. Control of catastrophic bleeding — with manual compression and resuscitation
  2. Ensure that injury is not causing airway compromise
  3. Evaluate the neurological status of patient — have a thorough baseline neuro exam and look for signs of ischemia, hemispheric stroke, arousability/mental status
  4. Prevent secondary injury — maintain blood pressure to maintain cerebral perfusion pressure and prevent hypoxia

Blunt Carotid Injury Patterns

  • Mechanism of Injury: history of a motor vehicle collision, blunt force to neck, severe hyperextension, etc.
  • External signs of injury: marks on neck or around the thoracic inlet (ie “seatbelt sign”))
  • Unilateral neurodeficits — could suggest hemispheric ischemia or stroke (less common, but possible)
  • Accompanying Injuries: concomitant closed head injuries (TBIs), complex facial fractures, cervical vertebral body and transverse foramen fractures
